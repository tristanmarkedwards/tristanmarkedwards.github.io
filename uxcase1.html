Creating a better return on investment for online merchants

Eccommerce merchants and marketeers are being held back by their own product feeds, and while many are generally aware the problem exists, they often don't know how or where to take action.


"I want to improve my return on investment by getting the absolute maximum out of my ad spend"







WHO IS THE USER?


Young professionals, male or female, often running their own online business as a 'side gig'



While they may do their own online shopping as a customer as well, their primary interest is improving the visibility of their own product listings to improve sales. They may have more than one sales category, ranging from clothes, to electronics, to books, and beyond. You'll most often see them using a laptop for business purposes.









USER CHALLENGES


In order to do some initial research, I did a series of interviews with some of WakeupData's existing customer base. The existing customers use their other eccommerce application, WakeupData Hub, that can deploy their data feeds to different channels (Shopify, Facebook, Google Shopping, etc)


"I have no overview of why one product sells better than the other"


"It's not immediately obvious how I can improve the visibility of my products"


"There's no simple, straight forward way for me to compare the pricing in my product feed against other products in the market"









HIGH LEVEL PROPOSAL


After the initial customer interview round, myself and the CTO began throwing around ideas for a 'sister' application that could potentially work in tandem with WakeupData Hub. The controlling concept was that this sister app would give the customer very specific suggestions on what they could change to the product data in their data feed to improve search performance. The technical trade off being, that in order to give precise suggestions, the data type would have to be consistent, meaning the suggestions would be based against Google Shopping feeds (who had a 31.6% market share at the end of 2019) specifically.

Our new CEO at the time had also suggested that we explore the possibility of using a different branding for the product, as it did not neccessarily have to be sold in conjunction with WakeupData Hub. After a product naming competition, the team settled on 'WiseMetrics'.









DESIGN


In trying to get a feel for the product, I produced several logo variations, including With the intial research completed and the high level proposal for a technical solution out of the way, it was time for me to work with the engineers on a workflow for the application.




With the theme of it being a 'sister application', I wanted to try and make a connection to the design I'd earlier produce for WakeupData Hub, by laying out core components (eg. header, breadcrumb trail, sub-navigation, user setting controls) in similar or the same locations.

An example of the existing WakeupData Hub user interface


My initial wireframe design for the 'suggestions' page of WiseMetrics. This was forseen to be the page the user would see on every visit, and how they would start their workflow.



A higher fidelity mockup of the 'suggestions' page, as it would be seen on a tablet device - the decidedly lowest supported resolution device. I used a combination of Google Analytics and UserStory data from our existing Hub userbase to look at what devices our customers used, and was surprised to find a high number of tablet users performing complex tasks.



What is Usability Testing? Image taken from ProductPlan

I reached out to a number of our customers who have frequent and lengthy user sessions (as identified on UserStory) to see if they'd be willing to participate in a guerilla usability testing session. I wanted them to undertake a quick 5 step process to see if they could successfully complete a common workflow - 'making a change to a product with a highlighted issue'.


The testing session was largely successful in proving the flow was inituitive, but a frequent point that came up in communication with the users was some general mistaking and confusion about our two products.

"What is WakeupData and what is WiseMetrics? Aren't they the same?"



It occurred to me that the similarity between the interface layouts had created some unintended sense of ambiguity that might need to be addressed.



I figured, what if we presented the applications as more of a 'yin and yan'? One with a top navigation, the other a side navigation. Perhaps even different colour palette approaches - soft colours vs high contrast colours, full-size logo vs iconised logo, a more conventional layout vs more dynamic. This fed also into the concept of building a separate brand from the already established WakeupData.


As with all my application design work, I tested the different colour combinations a user can experience throughout the workflows to assess their readability, and to ensure the application colour scheme would meet the Web Content Accessibility Guidelines (WCAG).
